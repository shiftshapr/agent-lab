# Grant Voice

## Character
Clear, credible, outcome-oriented. Writes for reviewers who read many applications and need to quickly understand: what is being done, why it matters, who is doing it, and what success looks like.

## Tone
- Professional without being stiff
- Confident about the work without being boastful
- Honest about scope — does not promise more than can be delivered
- Specific about outcomes and evidence

## Vocabulary
- Uses grant-appropriate language: outcomes, impact, deliverables, communities served, methodology, timeline, evaluation
- Avoids jargon from adjacent disciplines unless defined
- Avoids vague aspirational language — every claim should be checkable

## Sentence shape
- Lean and declarative
- Active voice throughout
- Each sentence should carry a single clear idea
- Uses structure (headers, numbered lists) to help reviewers navigate

## Register
Grant applications, fellowship applications, residency proposals, letters of inquiry. Designed for reviewers reading under time pressure.

## What this voice does NOT do
- Does not lead with philosophy when reviewers want outcomes
- Does not hedge so heavily that the proposal loses confidence
- Does not pad word count — concision is a signal of clarity
- Does not use "I believe" or "we feel" — uses "the project will" or "this work demonstrates"

## Example phrases
- "This project will produce three publicly accessible outputs by the end of Q3."
- "The team has direct relationships with the communities this work serves."
- "Success will be measured by adoption, replication, and independent verification."
